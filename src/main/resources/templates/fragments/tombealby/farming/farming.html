Farming Memories

I grew up on a family farm at Surfleet in South Lincolnshire. It was an arable farm growing cereals, potatoes, sugar beet and vegetables. The following are some pictorial memories of life on the farm.

The Fifties, Sixties and Seventies

The farm got its first combine harvester in 1950. The combine needed two people to operate it. As well as the driver, someone had to bag up the grain and drop the full bags down a shute for later collection.

Potatoes were picked by hand in the 1950's. This was back breaking work. Here we see the harvesting gang taking a well earned rest and some refreshments.

By the early 1960's potato growing was becoming more mechanised. In the photo on the left below, potatoes are being planted. The tractor driving towards the camera is pulling a cultivator. The cultivator is breaking up the big lumps in the soil to create a fine tilth. Creating a fine tilth helped crop growth during the season and made harvesting easier later in the year. The machine on the left in the same photo, which is driving away from the camera, is planting potatoes.

The farm grew vining peas for a few years in the 1970s. The peas were sold to Birds Eye to make frozen peas. It was vital that the peas got to the pea factory as soon as possible after they were picked. Here we see a photo of a trailer of peas being tipped into a lorry. The lorry will shortly be on its way to the pea factory.

The Eighties and Nineties

Onion drilling was one of the first outside jobs of the year. We would drill any time from the middle of March onwards, providing the soil conditions were suitable. Tiny onions seeds are fragile, so its important that they are planted into a soil that has a fine tilth. If this doesn't happen the seeds can die through lack of moisture. The drill coulters blocked occasionally. Someone rode on the back of the drill to check that the seed was flowing correctly.

Things didn't always go to plan on the farm. Here is a photo of a cart of seed potatoes ready to take out to the fields for potato planting. The seed potatoes are contained in plastic trays. The trays have been stacked in columns on the cart. Unfortunately the trays weren't roped onto the cart securely enough. A bump in the road has caused the stacks to fall over. To clarify things, a 'seed' potato isn't literally a seed. Its a potato kept from the previous year's crop, which was too small to eat.

The seed potatoes were stored in trays over winter to allow the potatoes to start sprouting. The aim was for each seed potato to have a short strong sprout at the time that it was planted. This effectively extended the growing season and resulted in more potatoes being harvested in the autumn. Tipping the potatoes out of the trays into the potato planter was a labour intensive job. This is what is being done in the photo below.

The following photo shows potatoes being planted. Potato planting took place straight after the onions were drilled.

The farm was able to irrigate from the mid 1980s. This was a big advantage if the season was dry. However irrigating was expensive, and only crops with potential high value like potatoes and onions justified the expense. Here we can see potatoes being irrigated.

The combine harvesters got bigger and more reliable as time passed. The photos below show a combine that we bought second hand at a local farm sale in 1990. The combine was made by the American firm John Deere. It was over 10 years old when we bought it, but was a very reliable machine and we used it for several years.

Potato picking was our busiest time of the year. The potato harvest started in middle of September. If the harvest went well we would finish picking around the middle of October. However the harvest was very weather dependent. If the weather turned wet, the soil turned to mud and stuck to the potatoes as they were lifted out of the ground. Sorting the mud from the potatoes was hard work for both the potato harvester machine and for the people working on the harvester.

Sugar beet harvesting machinery is big and expensive. We did not grow enough sugar beet to justify owning our own sugar beet harvester. Therefore we paid a local contractor to harvest our sugar beet. The photo below shows the sugar beet being harvested.

The Noughties

Back in the day I used to go to work in a boiler suit and a silly hat. Here's a family photo from about the year 2000.

I worked on the farm for many years. But by 2001 I had realised that I wasn't very good at farming and that I wasn't enjoying it either. It was time for someone else to have a go. The following photos are from the farm machinery sale that we held in March 2002. This brought to an end one hundred and fifty years of the Bealbys farming at Surfleet.
